## The Problem

Every day, NAU's dining halls — Hotspot, Dub's, Coops, Pizza Hut, Cobrizo — end up with surplus food. Most of it gets composted. Composting isn't wrong, exactly. But a few miles away, and sometimes a few dorms away, students and community members are dealing with food insecurity. Two problems sitting right next to each other, unconnected.

**Surplus to Serve** is a project management plan to connect them — a real system for collecting, safely handling, and redistributing that surplus food, built the way an actual project gets built: charter first, then scope, then schedule, then risk, then people, then communication. This repo documents that full plan, deliverable by deliverable, exactly as it was developed across the semester.

A note on authorship: this was a four-person team project. All four names appear across these deliverables, and I served as **Project Manager**, leading overall coordination — signing the charter, running the communication plan, and driving the team's weekly cadence. Where a specific deliverable was led by a teammate, that's noted honestly rather than smoothed over.

## Chapter 1 — The Charter

Every project starts with a document that answers one question: *why does this exist, and who's accountable for it?* The charter lays out the purpose (reduce food waste, fight food insecurity, build sustainability into campus dining), five concrete deliverables (a redistribution model, food safety guidelines, a composting fallback, a volunteer framework, and impact tracking), and a real budget: roughly **$7,000–$8,000** in one-time setup costs, plus recurring monthly costs.

It also names the risks up front, before any work begins: dining units might not participate, volunteers might not show up, food safety could go wrong. Naming those on page one, not page ten, is what makes a charter useful instead of decorative.

## Chapter 2 — Scope and the Work Breakdown Structure

A charter says *why*. A scope statement says *exactly what, and just as importantly, what not*. Surplus to Serve's scope is deliberately narrow: on-campus only, pre-packaged safe-to-distribute food only, no cooking, no off-campus sourcing, no home delivery. That narrowness is a feature — it's what keeps a food-safety-sensitive project actually achievable in one semester.

From there, the Work Breakdown Structure decomposes the whole project into five phases — Initiation, Planning, Execution, Monitoring & Control, and Closure — each broken down to task level. It's the difference between "make this happen" and an actual, checkable list of what "making this happen" requires.

## Chapter 4 — Who Owns What

A plan with no ownership is just a wish list. The RACI matrix assigns Responsible / Accountable / Consulted / Informed across every task in the WBS, against six roles: Project Manager, Sponsor, Key Stakeholder, the Project Team, Dining Services, and Student Organizations & Volunteers. It's what turns "the team will handle food safety" into "the Project Team is Responsible, the Sponsor is Accountable, and Dining Services is Consulted" — specific enough to actually run a weekly standup against.

## Chapter 5 — What Could Go Wrong

<img src="assets/risk_register_chart.png" width="600"/>

Ten risks, each scored by probability × impact, each with a named mitigation. The top-scoring risk — **dining unit non-participation** (score: 20) — makes sense: the entire project depends on food actually showing up. The mitigation wasn't hope, it was relationship-building and incentive design. Lower down, things like tracking-tool failure got a low-tech backup: a manual tracking process, because a $150 Google Form breaking shouldn't be able to stop food from reaching people.

📄 [Read the full Risk Register →](reports/05_Risk_Register.pdf)

## Chapter 6 — Knowing Who's in the Room

The stakeholder analysis maps eleven distinct groups — from the sponsor and dining units to health & safety officials and the food recipients themselves — across power, interest, and awareness. It's easy to plan for the obvious stakeholders (dining units, volunteers) and forget the quieter ones. This analysis explicitly tracks the Sustainability/Composting team and Health & Safety officials too, because a food redistribution project that ignores either one doesn't survive contact with reality.

📄 [Read the full Stakeholder Analysis →](reports/06_Stakeholder_Analysis.pdf)

## Chapter 7 — Keeping Everyone in the Loop

The communication matrix is the operational glue: who hears what, how often, through which channel, and delivered by whom. Bi-weekly team meetings. Monthly sponsor status reports. Weekly awareness campaigns on Instagram and campus flyers for student beneficiaries. Weekly transportation coordination over email and WhatsApp. Nothing here is generic — every audience got a communication plan actually shaped to how that audience operates.

📄 [Read the full Communication Matrix →](reports/07_Communication_Matrix.pdf)

## Chapter 8 — Reporting Progress

Two formal progress reports tracked Surplus to Serve against its plan mid-semester — partner engagement underway, requirements gathered, budget on track, overall status reported **green**. These aren't just status decks; they're the accountability loop that separates a plan that gets executed from a plan that gets filed away.

## What This Project Demonstrates

- **Full PM lifecycle fluency** — charter through closure, not just one slice of it
- **Risk-first thinking** — risks named in the charter, quantified in the register, mitigated with specific action, not generic caution
- **Stakeholder-aware planning** — eleven distinct groups mapped, including the easy-to-forget ones
- **Real budgeting discipline** — actual dollar estimates, not placeholders
- **Team leadership** — coordinated a four-person team as Project Manager across a full semester
